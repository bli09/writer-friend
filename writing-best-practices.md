# Writing Best Practices

## Fundamentals

**Clarity above all.** Every sentence should have one job. If you can cut a word without losing meaning, cut it. Readers should never have to re-read a sentence to understand it.

**Lead with the point.** State the most important idea first. Don't bury the thesis. Journalists call this the inverted pyramid — most critical information at the top, supporting detail below.

**Active voice.** "The team launched the product" beats "The product was launched by the team." Active voice is faster, clearer, and more authoritative.

**Concrete over abstract.** Replace vague words (things, aspects, leverage, synergy) with specific ones. "We cut costs by 30%" beats "We optimized our financial performance."

---

## Structure

**Hook immediately.** The first sentence must earn the reader's attention. Use a surprising fact, a bold claim, a vivid scene, or a direct address to the reader's problem.

**One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph has a topic sentence, supporting evidence, and a transition or conclusion. Never let a paragraph do two jobs.

**Use white space.** Short paragraphs and line breaks make writing easier to scan. Dense walls of text lose readers.

**End with intention.** The closing line should feel earned — a call to action, a restatement of stakes, or a memorable image. Never just stop.

---

## Tone & Voice
**Sound human.** It is very very important that this piece of writing doesn't sound like AI wrote it. 

**Match the audience.** A technical white paper and a cold email require different registers. Write at the level your reader expects — no higher, no lower.

**Be direct, not abrasive.** Confidence reads well. Arrogance doesn't. State your point without hedging ("I think maybe…"), but stay respectful of the reader's intelligence.

**Avoid jargon unless it earns its place.** Industry terms are fine when writing for experts. For general audiences, define or replace them.

**Read it aloud.** If you stumble reading it, rewrite it. Good prose has rhythm. If a sentence is hard to say, it's hard to read.

---

## Persuasion

**Show, don't just tell.** Back every claim with evidence — data, anecdotes, examples, comparisons. Assertions without proof are just opinions.

**Acknowledge objections.** Address the strongest counterargument before the reader raises it. This builds trust and strengthens your position.

**Use the rule of three.** Three examples, three benefits, three reasons — the human brain responds to triads. It feels complete.

**Create stakes.** Tell the reader what they gain by reading and acting, and what they risk by ignoring you. Make the outcome feel real.

---

## Editing

**Write first, edit second.** Never edit while drafting. Get the full piece out, then cut.

**Kill your darlings.** If you love a sentence but it doesn't serve the piece, delete it.

**Cut filler phrases.** Remove: "In order to", "It is important to note that", "At the end of the day", "Due to the fact that", "As previously mentioned."

**Check the opening and closing last.** They carry the most weight. Rewrite them after the body is solid.

**One final read for flow.** Does each paragraph connect to the next? Does the piece build? Does the ending pay off the opening?

---

## Format-Specific Notes

**Emails:** Subject line is everything. Body should be scannable — short paragraphs, one clear ask, specific CTA. For professional and work emails, use collaborative framing over directive framing. Prefer "we should", "I'd love to", "let's" over "we need to", "you must", "this requires". Assertive and direct is the goal; aggressive or accusatory is not.

**Reports / White papers:** Executive summary first. Use headers generously. Readers will skip around — make every section self-contained.

**Social / Short-form:** First line must hook without context. Write for the scroll. Every word competes with infinite content.

**Long-form / Essays:** Earn trust early with a strong thesis. Vary sentence length for rhythm. The ending should reframe the opening in a new light.

**Presentations / Speeches:** Write for the ear, not the eye. Shorter sentences. Repetition is a tool, not a flaw. End on an emotion, not a fact.

# Additional notes

**Don't use hyphens or dashes":** Don't use hyphens or dashes unless absolutely necessary

**Ban structural "bookending":**  Don’t include introductory "throat-clearing": Ban phrases like "In the rapidly evolving landscape of..." or "It is important to consider..."

**Don't use summary or conclusion sections:** Unless specifically requested, tell it to stop writing a final paragraph that starts with "In summary," or "Ultimately," which just repeats the previous points.

**Don't use passive voice"** Don’t use the passive voice: Instead of "The decision was made to..." insist on "We decided to..."

# Do NOT use these words
Delve, Tapestry, Labyrinth, Testament
Beacon, Unleash, Elevate, Harness, Empower, Synergy, Pivotal, Robust, Seamless, Innovative, Cutting-edge, Transformative, Comprehensive, Dynamic, Versatile, Nuanced

# Do NOT use these phrases
"This means that…", "As a result…", "One key benefit is…", "Another important factor is…", "This highlights the importance of…", "It's important to note", "It's worth mentioning", "Generally speaking", "In many cases", "To some extent"
