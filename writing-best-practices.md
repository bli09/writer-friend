# Writing Best Practices

## Writing for Executives

**BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front).** State the ask, decision, or conclusion in the first sentence. Context goes below. Executives decide whether to keep reading in under 5 seconds.

**One ask per message.** Two asks get half-answered. Send two messages.

**Signal time cost upfront.** Lead the body with it. "60-second read." "Need 5 minutes." "No reading required if you trust me on this."

**Quantify everything.** "Saves 3 days per release" beats "speeds things up." "$2.4M ARR at risk" beats "significant revenue impact." Specific numbers signal you've done the work.

**Frame in their terms.** Cost, time, risk, revenue, competitive position. Translate engineering work into business outcomes: not "we refactored the auth service" but "login latency dropped 40%, cutting support tickets by ~20 per week."

**Date and scope everything.** "By next week" is ambiguous. "By Fri Oct 25" is not. "Customer impact" is vague. "Affects 12 enterprise accounts including Acme and Brex" is concrete.

**Make the tradeoff explicit.** When proposing, show the cost of inaction. "If we delay 3 weeks, we miss the Q3 launch window. Competitors A and B are already announced."

**Default to a proposal, not a question.** "I'd like to do X. Flag concerns by Thursday" reduces decision friction. "Should we do X?" creates work for them.

**Yes/no questions beat open ones.** When you need a decision, ask "Approve the $50K spend?" not "Thoughts on the spend?" Yes/no questions are easy to answer, easy to track, and create accountability. Reserve open-ended questions for true exploration.

**Use forcing functions for decisions.** "Going ahead with X on Monday unless you flag concerns by Friday EOD" converts a request into an opt-out. It respects everyone's time and gets decisions faster. Use only for things within your authority.

## Choosing the Medium

How you write matters less than where you send it. The wrong medium kills the message no matter how well it's written. Match the medium to the stakes.

**Bad news, conflict, or anything emotional.** In person if you can. On a call if not. Never email. Tone gets lost in text; trust dies in writing.

**Praise and recognition.** In public. Slack channel, all-hands, public reply on a doc. Private praise is half-discarded; public praise builds reputation for both of you.

**Decisions.** In writing (email or doc). You need a searchable record of who agreed to what.

**Brainstorming and quick reactions.** Slack or thread. Don't email half-formed thoughts.

**Status and progress.** Async (doc, dashboard, Slack post). Don't pull people into meetings for things they could read in 90 seconds.

An exquisitely crafted email delivering bad news lands worse than a clumsy phone call. Pick the medium before you pick the words.

## Fundamentals

**Clarity above all.** Every sentence should have one job. If you can cut a word without losing meaning, cut it. Readers should never have to re-read a sentence to understand it.

**Active voice.** "The team launched the product" beats "The product was launched by the team." Active voice is faster, clearer, and more authoritative.

**Concrete over abstract.** Replace vague words (things, aspects, leverage, synergy) with specific ones. "We cut costs by 30%" beats "We optimized our financial performance."

## Structure

**Lead with what grabs them.** For business messages, that's the ask or decision (see BLUF in Writing for Executives). For essays, social posts, and narrative writing, that's a hook: surprise, specificity, or stakes that earn the next sentence.

**One idea per paragraph.** Each paragraph has a topic sentence, supporting evidence, and a transition or conclusion. Never let a paragraph do two jobs.

**Use white space.** Short paragraphs and line breaks make writing easier to scan. Dense walls of text lose readers.

**End with the next action.** The closing line names who does what by when. Make it a verb-led commitment or request: "I'll send the draft by Thursday EOD" or "Can you approve by Tuesday standup?" Save memorable closes for essays and speeches.

## Cognitive Load

**Optimize for the skimmer.** Assume the reader will only read the bold words and the first sentence of each paragraph. If the message still lands, you've done it right.

**Bold the asks and the numbers.** Make the most important phrase in each section impossible to miss.

**Use bullets for parallel information.** Three reasons, three options, three risks. List them. Don't paragraph them.

**Alternate sentence length.** Most sentences 8 to 15 words. Drop in shorter ones (3 to 6 words) for emphasis. Avoid stacking 25-word sentences.

**One concept per sentence.** If two ideas are joined by "and," split them.

**Headers carry meaning.** "Update" is a wasted header. "Decision needed: pricing tier for SMB" is not.

## Tone & Voice

**Sound human.** Don't write anything that sounds like AI wrote it. This matters more than any other rule.

**Conviction over hedging.** "I'm going to do X" beats "I'd like to try X." "I will deliver Thursday" beats "I'm aiming for Thursday." Hedging signals you're asking permission to fail. Commit first; adjust if reality changes.

**Be direct, not abrasive.** Confidence reads well. Arrogance doesn't. State your point clearly while staying respectful of the reader's intelligence.

**Match the audience and mirror their vocabulary.** A technical white paper and a cold email require different registers. Write at the level your reader expects, no higher and no lower. Use the actual words your reader uses in their own emails, decks, and Slack. If they say "GTM motion," don't say "go-to-market strategy." Mirroring signals attention and builds rapport faster than any other tactic.

**Avoid jargon unless it earns its place.** Industry terms are fine when writing for experts. For general audiences, define or replace them.

**Read it aloud.** If you stumble reading it, rewrite it. Good prose has rhythm. If a sentence is hard to say, it's hard to read.

## Persuasion

**Show, don't just tell.** Back every claim with evidence: data, anecdotes, examples, comparisons. Assertions without proof are just opinions.

**Use the rule of three.** Three examples, three benefits, three reasons. The human brain responds to triads. It feels complete.

**Create stakes.** Tell the reader what they gain by reading and acting, and what they risk by ignoring you. Make the outcome feel real.

**Acknowledge objections.** Address the strongest counterargument before the reader raises it. This builds trust and strengthens your position.

**Pre-mortem your proposals.** When proposing anything significant, name how it could fail in the same message. "Biggest risk: Acme churns over the price increase. Mitigation: grandfather them at current rate." Articulating your own failure modes builds more trust than projecting pure confidence. Executives trust people who can see clearly, not people who only see upside.

**Don't restate when you disagree.** After you've made your case once and the reader has disagreed, restating doesn't help. It escalates. Move to "what would change your mind?" or accept and execute. People who repeat the same argument louder lose more influence than people who concede gracefully.

## Editing

**Write first, edit second.** Never edit while drafting. Get the full piece out, then cut.

**Kill your darlings.** If you love a sentence but it doesn't serve the piece, delete it.

**Cut filler phrases.** Remove: "In order to," "It is important to note that," "At the end of the day," "Due to the fact that," "As previously mentioned."

**Check the opening and closing last.** They carry the most weight. Rewrite them after the body is solid.

**One final read for flow.** Does each paragraph connect to the next? Does the piece build? Does the ending pay off the opening?

**The 24-hour rule for hot messages.** Never send a message written in anger, hurt, or frustration on the same day. Save the draft. Re-read tomorrow with fresh eyes. If you'd still send it, send a softer version. The cost of a regretted email to a peer or boss is measured in months of trust.

## Format-Specific Notes

**Emails to executives.** Subject line carries the ask. Use formats like "Decision needed: pricing tier," "FYI: incident postmortem," or "Action by Thu: contract review." Open with the ask, not pleasantries; skip "hope this finds you well." Bold the ask and any dates. One CTA per email: decision, intro, or feedback. For professional and work emails, use collaborative framing over directive framing. Prefer "we should," "I'd love to," "let's" over "we need to," "you must," "this requires." Direct is the goal; aggressive is not.

**Slack and async messages.** Lead with the ask, not the context. "Need your call on X. Here's why:" beats "Here's what's happening, and so..." One topic per message. Use threads for follow-up. Bold timing words: **today**, **EOD**, **Thursday**. If it runs longer than 4 lines, consider whether it should be a doc instead.

**Reports and white papers.** Executive summary first. Use headers generously. Readers will skip around, so make every section stand on its own.

**Social and short-form.** First line must hook without context. Write for the scroll. Every word competes with infinite content.

**Long-form and essays.** Earn trust early with a strong thesis. Vary sentence length for rhythm. The ending should reframe the opening in a new light.

**Presentations and speeches.** Write for the ear, not the eye. Shorter sentences. Repetition is a tool, not a flaw. End on an emotion, not a fact.

## What to Avoid

### Structural patterns

**Hyphens and dashes.** Avoid them unless absolutely necessary.

**Structural bookending.** No introductory throat clearing. Cut phrases like "In the rapidly evolving landscape of..." or "It is important to consider..."

**Summary or conclusion sections.** Unless specifically requested, don't end with paragraphs that start with "In summary" or "Ultimately." They just repeat what came before.

### Words

Delve, Tapestry, Labyrinth, Testament, Beacon, Unleash, Elevate, Harness, Empower, Synergy, Pivotal, Robust, Seamless, Innovative, Cutting-edge, Transformative, Comprehensive, Dynamic, Versatile, Nuanced

### Phrases

"This means that...", "As a result...", "One key benefit is...", "Another important factor is...", "This highlights the importance of...", "It's important to note", "It's worth mentioning", "Generally speaking", "In many cases", "To some extent"

### Corporate clichés

These appear constantly in tech corporate writing and signal lazy thinking:

- "Circle back" / "Touch base." Say when and about what instead.
- "Per my last email." Passive aggressive. Just resend or quote.
- "Going forward." Usually deletable. If not, say "starting Monday."
- "Bandwidth." Say "capacity," or better, name what's actually blocking.
- "Quick win" / "Low-hanging fruit." Name the actual work.
- "Move the needle." Name the metric.
- "Boil the ocean." Name the scope you'd cut.
- "Take this offline." Usually means "I don't want to answer." Use sparingly.
- "Reach out." Use "ask," "email," or "call." Pick the verb that matches the action.
- "Stakeholders." Name the actual people if you can.
